Ido Nitzan Hidekel

Ph.D. direct-track student at Tel Aviv University (Geometric Deep Learning Lab) · Lead, Deep Learning at Dtect Vision.

I work on the foundations of deep learning — spectral bias, optimization dynamics, and generalization — and how those foundations inform the design of practical systems that detect AI-generated media. My approach sits at the intersection of signal processing and deep learning: I treat learned representations as signals, ask which frequencies a network can and cannot see, and turn those answers into architectures, loss functions, and training procedures that expose artifacts modern generators leave behind.

At Dtect Vision I lead the deep learning group: we translate theoretical insights about frequency-domain behavior of neural networks into production-grade detectors for generated images and audio, with an emphasis on cross-generator generalization and robustness under deployment shift. Academically, I am advised in the Geometric Deep Learning Lab, working on spectral-bias-aware optimization and frequency-guided representations.

Prior: B.Sc. in Applied Mathematics and M.Sc. in Electrical Engineering (signal processing track), both at Tel Aviv University. I moved into the direct Ph.D. track after completing my M.Sc. thesis work.

SONAR: Spectral-Contrastive Audio Residuals for Generalizable Deepfake Detection

Ido Nitzan Hidekel, Gal Lifshitz, Khen Cohen, Dan Raviv
arXiv preprint, Nov 2025 · under submission to ICML 2026

Neural networks preferentially learn low frequencies, which both leaves high-frequency artifacts in generated audio and leaves those artifacts under-exploited by common detectors. SONAR is a dual-path framework that learns data-driven SRM-style filters to isolate high-frequency residuals and uses a Jensen–Shannon contrastive objective to pull real content/noise pairs together and push fake pairs apart in latent space, yielding strong generalization to unseen generators.

Research Agendain brief

Spectral bias & optimization

When and why networks under-represent high-frequency structure during training, and how optimizer choice, parameterization, and loss geometry shape that behavior across training.

Generalization in detectors

Why synthetic-media detectors transfer poorly across generators, and what inductive biases (frequency residuals, contrastive structure, architectural constraints) restore cross-domain generalization.

Signal processing meets deep learning

Using classical signal-processing tools — subband decompositions, SRM-style high-pass filters, Fourier / wavelet analysis — as first-class components inside modern networks.

Audio & image forensics in the wild

Moving detection results from clean benchmarks to deployed conditions: codecs, compression, domain shift, adversarial pressure, and the evaluation protocols needed to see those failures.
